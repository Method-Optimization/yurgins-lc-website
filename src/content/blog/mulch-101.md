Fresh mulch is one of the highest-impact things you can do for a property. It locks in moisture, suppresses weeds, regulates soil temperature, and gives every bed a clean, finished look. But there's a right way to do it — and a few easy ways to get it wrong.

## How deep should mulch be?

Aim for **2 to 3 inches**. That's enough to block sunlight from weed seeds and hold moisture, without suffocating roots. Less than two inches and weeds push right through; more than three and you can starve the soil of oxygen and trap too much water.

## How much do you need?

Mulch is sold by the cubic yard. One cubic yard covers about **100 square feet at 3 inches deep**. So measure your beds (length × width), multiply for square footage, and divide by 100 for a rough yard count at 3 inches. Round up — it's better to have a little extra than to come up short halfway through.

## The mistakes we fix most

- **Mulch volcanoes.** Piling mulch up against tree trunks traps moisture and rots the bark. Keep it pulled back a few inches from trunks and stems.
- **Mulching over weeds.** Mulch suppresses *seeds*, not established weeds. Pull them first.
- **Never refreshing.** Old mulch breaks down, fades, and compacts. A light annual refresh keeps beds working and looking sharp.

## When to refresh

Once a year is plenty for most South Jersey beds — spring is ideal. If the color has faded and the layer has thinned below two inches, it's time. You usually don't need to remove the old mulch; just top it off to the right depth.

Bed prep, clean edges, even placement, and the right depth — that's the difference between mulch that looks good for a few weeks and mulch that works all season.
